Undergraduate Research

Pedestrian Routing Optimization Using GIS

Role: Undergraduate Researcher

Research Area: Pedestrian routing, spatial network analysis

Methods: Multi-objective optimization, GIS-based modeling

Tools: Python, GIS software, spatial network libraries

Research Motivation

Abstract

Levee systems protect cities from flooding but often act as barriers that hinder local mobility. This research proposes an integrated multi-objective optimization and spatial analysis framework for selecting levee crossing facilities in riverfront districts of New Taipei City, Taiwan.

The framework simultaneously considers construction and sidewalk improvement costs, population coverage, spatial equity, and traffic safety by incorporating GIS data, population distributions, and crash records. Candidate locations are evaluated using both NSGA-III and simulated quantum annealing (SQA) implemented on dedicated hardware.

Results reveal explicit trade-offs among cost, accessibility, equity, and safety, while highlighting underserved and high-risk areas. The comparative analysis demonstrates that SQA achieves performance comparable to NSGA-III and shows promise for scaling complex infrastructure siting problems in urban planning.

Pedestrian routing plays a critical role in urban mobility, accessibility, and safety analysis. Conventional routing approaches often prioritize shortest paths or minimum travel time, which may fail to capture pedestrian-specific considerations such as perceived safety, walkability, and accessibility. This research aims to develop a routing framework that better reflects pedestrian decision-making in urban environments.

Research Objectives

  • To represent pedestrian networks using GIS-based spatial graph models.
  • To incorporate multiple routing criteria beyond distance and time.
  • To analyze trade-offs among competing routing objectives.

Methodology

The pedestrian routing problem is formulated as a multi-objective optimization problem on a spatial network. Each network segment is associated with multiple attributes, such as geometric distance, safety indicators, and accessibility measures. Optimization techniques are applied to identify efficient routes under different preference scenarios.

Research Contributions

  • A GIS-based framework for multi-criteria pedestrian routing analysis.
  • Integration of spatial data and optimization models for decision support.
  • Systematic evaluation of routing outcomes under varying objective weights.

Ongoing and Future Directions

Ongoing work explores extensions to dynamic routing under congestion and uncertainty, as well as the application of advanced optimization approaches, including heuristic and quantum-inspired methods, to large-scale pedestrian networks.
