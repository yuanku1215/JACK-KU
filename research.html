Abstract

Levee systems protect cities from floods, but they can also act as mobility barriers that fragment neighborhoods and reduce access to daily services. In riverfront districts such as Sanchong and Luzhou in New Taipei City, residents may experience longer detours and uneven service accessibility due to insufficient levee crossings.

This research develops an integrated framework that combines GIS-based spatial analysis with multi-objective optimization to support the selection of levee crossing facilities. The model simultaneously considers construction and sidewalk improvement costs, population coverage, spatial equity, and traffic safety by incorporating road network data, population distributions, and crash records.

Candidate locations are generated from spatial datasets and evaluated using two complementary optimization approaches: the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm III (NSGA-III) for Pareto front approximation, and simulated quantum annealing (SQA) via QUBO encoding to explore large-scale combinatorial search spaces. The resulting solutions reveal explicit trade-offs among cost, accessibility, equity, and safety, enabling interpretable planning insights for people-centered urban infrastructure decision-making.

Takeaway: This project is not ‚Äújust shortest path.‚Äù It is a multi-objective, city-scale siting problem where improving accessibility may conflict with budget, equity, and safety ‚Äî making it a strong fit for Pareto optimization and QUBO/SQA experimentation.

Motivation & Research Questions

Traditional planning approaches often emphasize feasibility and cost, but may not explicitly quantify who benefits and who is left behind. For levee-crossing planning, this can lead to solutions that improve average accessibility while still maintaining pockets of underserved neighborhoods, especially where street connectivity and safe crossings are limited.

  • RQ1 ‚Äî Multi-objective planning: How can we jointly optimize cost, coverage, spatial equity, and safety in a single framework?
  • RQ2 ‚Äî Spatial insight: Which neighborhoods remain underserved under cost-driven solutions, and why?
  • RQ3 ‚Äî Solver comparison: Can QUBO-based simulated quantum annealing achieve competitive performance compared to NSGA-III as the problem scales?

Data & Study Setup

Demand (Population)

Demand is represented using spatial population distributions (e.g., grids or points) to measure coverage and equity. Metrics are computed using walkable-network accessibility rather than straight-line distance.

Network (Walkability)

The road network is used to compute travel distances and connectivity under realistic movement constraints, enabling interpretable accessibility outcomes.

Candidate Sites

Candidate crossing locations are generated along levee segments using GIS rules (geometry, spacing, and nearby network feasibility), producing a planning-ready set of selectable sites.

Safety (Crash Risk)

Traffic crash records are mapped into spatial risk indicators to penalize solutions that concentrate crossings near high-risk intersections or corridors.

Implementation note: what ‚Äúplanning-ready‚Äù means in this project
Candidate sites are designed to be usable for planning discussion: each site is (1) spatially valid along a levee segment, (2) connected to the surrounding street network, and (3) suitable for evaluating cost and sidewalk-improvement needs with GIS-based measurements.
  • Feasibility constraints help filter unrealistic sites early.
  • Network-based metrics keep evaluation consistent with real movement.
  • Safety and equity are modeled explicitly, not as afterthoughts.

Method: GIS ‚Üí Multi-objective Model ‚Üí Solvers

The framework follows a pipeline approach that turns raw spatial datasets into comparable decision metrics, then searches for non-dominated solutions under multiple conflicting goals. This structure supports both evolutionary Pareto search (NSGA-III) and QUBO-based optimization (SQA), enabling a principled comparison across solver paradigms.

Step 1 ‚Äî Spatial preprocessing

Generate candidate sites, compute walkable-network accessibility, and build spatial matrices for evaluation.

Step 2 ‚Äî Multi-objective formulation

Define objectives (cost, coverage, equity, safety) and planning constraints to reflect real-world feasibility.

Step 3 ‚Äî Solver execution

Run NSGA-III for Pareto approximation; run QUBO/SQA to explore combinatorial decision space.

Model formulation (human-readable)

Each candidate site corresponds to a binary decision variable (0/1). The model selects a subset of sites that jointly optimizes:

  • Cost: construction and sidewalk retrofit costs (minimize)
  • Coverage: population served within network-based accessibility thresholds (maximize)
  • Equity: reduce spatial disparity in service accessibility (maximize / minimize inequality)
  • Safety: avoid placing crossings in high-risk crash environments (minimize risk)

Constraints enforce planning feasibility, such as budget, spacing, and realistic implementation limits. This binary structure naturally supports QUBO encoding for SQA experiments.

Results & Insights

The primary outcome is a set of non-dominated solutions that explicitly visualize trade-offs between competing planning goals. Instead of a single ‚Äúbest‚Äù answer, the Pareto front provides a menu of options that can be selected based on policy preferences (e.g., equity-first vs cost-first).

  • Trade-offs are explicit: lower-cost solutions may concentrate benefits in already-served corridors, reducing equity.
  • Underserved areas become visible: some high-demand neighborhoods remain under-covered unless equity is prioritized.
  • Safety-aware siting matters: incorporating crash risk can shift recommendations away from high-risk corridors while maintaining accessibility gains.
  • Solver comparison: SQA produces competitive solution patterns relative to NSGA-III, suggesting promise for scalable combinatorial exploration.
Interpretation style (for planners): choose a solution by answering ‚ÄúWhat are we willing to trade?‚Äù ‚Äî a small increase in cost may produce a large equity gain, or a safer configuration may slightly reduce coverage but significantly lower exposure to crash risk.

Significance & Future Work

This project demonstrates how integrated spatial analysis and advanced optimization can support people-centered and resilient infrastructure planning. By translating planning goals into explicit multi-objective metrics, the framework offers both interpretability (maps, Pareto trade-offs) and computational scalability (QUBO/SQA experimentation).

  • Dynamic demand: extend the model to time-of-day or event-driven demand patterns.
  • Uncertainty-aware planning: incorporate stochastic risk models and robustness criteria.
  • Scaling experiments: expand candidate sets to city scale and benchmark GPU-accelerated optimization.
