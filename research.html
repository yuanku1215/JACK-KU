Research

Multi-objective Spatial Facility Location for Levee Crossing Planning

Role: Undergraduate Researcher

Study Area: Sanchong & Luzhou, New Taipei City, Taiwan

Research Topic: Levee crossing facility location

Methods: NSGA-III, Simulated Quantum Annealing (SQA)

Tools: Python, GIS, spatial network & population data

Abstract

Levee systems safeguard cities from floods but often function as barriers that disrupt local mobility. In riverfront districts such as Sanchong and Luzhou in New Taipei City, Taiwan, residents face limited access to services due to insufficient levee crossings. This research develops an integrated multi-objective optimization and spatial analysis framework for selecting levee crossing facilities.

The framework simultaneously considers construction and sidewalk improvement costs, population coverage, spatial equity, and traffic safety by incorporating GIS data, population distributions, and crash records. Candidate locations are generated from spatial datasets and evaluated using two optimization approaches: the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm III (NSGA-III) and simulated quantum annealing (SQA) executed on dedicated hardware.

Results reveal explicit trade-offs among cost, accessibility, equity, and safety, highlighting underserved and high-risk areas along levee systems. Comparative analysis shows that SQA achieves performance comparable to NSGA-III while demonstrating potential advantages in scaling complex spatial infrastructure siting problems.

Problem Definition

The placement of levee crossing facilities can be formulated as a spatial facility location problem. Given a set of candidate locations along levees, the objective is to select an appropriate subset that balances multiple, often conflicting, planning goals under spatial and budgetary constraints.

Optimization Framework

  • Candidate facility locations are generated using GIS-based spatial analysis of levee geometry and surrounding road networks.
  • Population coverage and equity metrics are derived from spatial population distributions.
  • Traffic crash records are incorporated to explicitly account for safety considerations.
  • NSGA-III is used to approximate the Pareto front of multi-objective solutions.
  • Simulated quantum annealing (SQA) is applied via QUBO encoding to explore large-scale combinatorial solution spaces.

Key Findings

  • Clear trade-offs exist between cost efficiency and spatial equity.
  • High-population areas are not always optimally served under cost-driven solutions.
  • Quantum-inspired optimization exhibits competitive performance relative to evolutionary algorithms.

Research Significance and Future Work

This research demonstrates how advanced multi-objective optimization and quantum-inspired methods can support people-centered and resilient urban infrastructure planning. Future work will extend the framework to dynamic demand scenarios, stochastic risk modeling, and GPU-accelerated optimization for city-scale applications.
