Overview

In this course, I practiced modeling repeatedly across many supply chain problem types. Each exercise followed the same professional workflow: define decision variables, write a clean objective function, express operational constraints, run the solver, and then interpret results in managerial terms.

Although each individual problem was not ‚Äúhard‚Äù by itself, the volume and variety of tasks built something more valuable: a complete modeling foundation. I learned how small formulation choices can silently change feasibility, optimality, and interpretation ‚Äî and how to debug models by inspecting constraints and objective behavior.

Core outcome: I can quickly move from a story-like description (business decision) to a formal optimization model ‚Äî and I understand that multiple mathematically equivalent forms can behave differently in implementation.

What I Built (Skills & Habits)

Model Formulation Literacy

Comfortable writing LP / ILP / MILP structure: variables, objective, constraints, indexing, and units consistency.

Constraint Debugging

Learned to detect hidden traps: missing bounds, wrong indexing, big-M misuse, and constraint redundancy/infeasibility.

Multi-language Thinking

Beyond Python, I developed solver-oriented thinking through LINGO syntax‚Äîtransferable to Pyomo/PuLP/OR-Tools.

Example: why ‚Äúequivalent math‚Äù can produce different results in code
In practice, I found that two formulations can look mathematically similar but behave differently due to:
  • implicit bounds vs explicit bounds
  • indexing mistakes (missing subset constraints)
  • big-M values that are too large or too small
  • objective scaling that affects numerical stability
This course trained me to verify formulations systematically before trusting solver outputs.

Model Patterns Library

I treat my collection of LINGO models as a ‚Äúpattern library.‚Äù Even with dozens of exercises, most fall into a small set of reusable modeling templates. This is the exact skill that helps in IEOR graduate work: recognizing structure and adapting it to new contexts.

1) Transportation / Transshipment

Min-cost flow with supply/demand constraints, capacity limits, and multi-stage shipping decisions.

2) Facility Location

Binary open/close decisions with assignment, fixed costs, and service constraints.

3) Production Planning

Multi-period decisions with inventory balance, production capacity, and backorder/holding costs.

4) Workforce / Resource Allocation

Shift coverage and assignment models; feasibility-first constraints with cost minimization.

5) Blending / Mixing

Quality constraints, ratio constraints, and cost minimization under composition limits.

6) Scheduling (Intro-level)

Start-time / precedence constraints and simplified machine capacity decisions.

Optional: a tiny LINGO-style pseudo-template I frequently used
SETS:
  I /.../: supply;
  J /.../: demand;
  IJ(I,J): x, cost;
ENDSETS

MIN = @SUM(IJ: cost(IJ) * x(IJ));
@FOR(I: @SUM(J: x(I,J)) <= supply(I));
@FOR(J: @SUM(I: x(I,J)) >= demand(J));
@FOR(IJ: x(IJ) >= 0);
This kind of structure made me fast at building clean models and debugging constraint logic.

From LINGO to Python (Transferable Skills)

Working with LINGO strengthened my ability to express optimization problems in a solver-friendly form. This directly transfers to Python-based modeling stacks such as PuLP, Pyomo, or OR-Tools, because the core logic is identical: indices, sets, constraints, and objective composition.

  • Same math, different syntax: modeling is the invariant; languages are interchangeable tools.
  • Cleaner structure: sets/indexing discipline helps avoid bugs in large Python models.
  • IEOR readiness: the repeated practice builds intuition for MILP, decomposition, and heuristics later.
