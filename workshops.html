Overview

The goal of this workshop was to give participants a practical and structured entry point into quantum algorithms, especially for optimization. Instead of starting from physics-heavy concepts, I organized the content around a familiar optimization narrative: energy landscapes, local minima, and search strategies.

The workshop progresses from classical annealing ideas to simulated annealing, then to quantum annealing, highlighting what changes (and what stays the same) when we reformulate optimization problems as QUBO. I also addressed common implementation challenges ‚Äî such as encoding constraints, scaling penalties, and debugging energy functions ‚Äî and concluded with demos and hands-on coding exercises.

Teaching design choice: build intuition first (optimization viewpoint), then introduce quantum concepts as extensions ‚Äî so participants can immediately connect the ideas to their own research problems.

Geomatics MS/PhD students and cross-department learners who want an applied view of quantum optimization.

Learning Goal

Outcome

Workshop Structure

The slide deck follows my own learning path ‚Äî designed to minimize ‚Äúquantum intimidation‚Äù and maximize transfer to real optimization tasks.

Part 1 ‚Äî Optimization Intuition

Energy landscapes, local minima, exploration vs exploitation, and why annealing works as a search principle.

Part 2 ‚Äî Simulated Annealing (SA)

Temperature schedules, acceptance probability, neighborhood design, and practical pitfalls when coding SA.

Part 3 ‚Äî Quantum Annealing (QA)

From classical states to quantum fluctuations, tunneling intuition, and mapping optimization to Ising/QUBO.

Part 4 ‚Äî QUBO Modeling & Practice

How to build QUBO from basic math, encode constraints, choose penalties, and validate energy correctness.

Common implementation issues I explicitly taught
  • Penalty tuning: too small ‚Üí infeasible solutions; too large ‚Üí numerical dominance and poor exploration.
  • Constraint encoding: equality constraints need careful quadratic expansion and scaling.
  • Energy debugging: verify that ‚Äúbetter solutions‚Äù truly reduce energy; test with known feasible cases.
  • Variable indexing: wrong mapping between (i, j, t) and bit indices can silently break the model.
  • Interpretability: always explain what each QUBO term means in the original problem context.

QUBO: From Basic Math to an Executable Model

A key part of the workshop focused on making QUBO feel ‚Äúconstructible‚Äù rather than mysterious. I showed how to start from simple binary variables, build objectives, and then add constraints as quadratic penalties. This gives a systematic recipe that participants can reuse.

Core message: QUBO is not magic ‚Äî it is a disciplined way to express a problem as min x·µÄQx with binary decisions, where every term has a clear meaning.
